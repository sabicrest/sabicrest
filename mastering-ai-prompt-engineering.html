HOW TO TALK TO AI
IN SIMPLE STEPS.

A helpful guide on how to ask AI for help so you get the best results every time.

1. What is Prompting?

"Prompting" is just a fancy word for talking to a computer. Imagine the AI is like a very smart student who has read every book in the library but doesn't know what *you* need right now. To get the best help, you have to be very clear.

Think of it like being a chef. The AI is your kitchen helper. If you say "Make food," they won't know if you want breakfast or dinner. But if you say "Make me a bowl of oatmeal with honey and bananas," you get exactly what you want. Prompting is the bridge that connects your ideas to the AI's power.

2. How the AI "Brain" Works

To be good at this, you need to know a secret: AI doesn't "think" the way we do. Instead, it plays a very advanced "guessing game."

Imagine a very fast reader who has memorized every book on Earth but has no "common sense." When you ask a question, the AI looks at all its books and tries to guess which word should come next.

If you are vague, the AI will make a vague guess. If you are specific, the AI will give a specific answer. This is why a student asking for "help with math" gets a different result than a student asking for "a step-by-step explanation of how to solve for X in this algebra problem."

3. The Simple "Secret Code" for Success

Many people get frustrated with AI because they write short, one-word questions. To get the best help, use this simple four-part formula:

  • 1. Give it a Role: Who should the AI be? (Example: "Act as a kind teacher" or "Be an expert mechanic.")
  • 2. Give it a Task: What is the job? (Example: "Write a letter to a customer.")
  • 3. Give it Rules: What are the limits? (Example: "Make it sound polite and use less than 100 words.")
  • 4. Give it a Format: How should the answer look? (Example: "Give me a list" or "Write it as a short story.")

4. Simple Tricks for Better Answers

4.1 Use the "Magic Phrase"

If you have a hard problem, add this sentence to your prompt: "Let's think step-by-step." This forces the AI to slow down and explain each part. It makes the AI much more accurate, especially with math or logic.

4.2 Show, Don't Just Tell

If you want the AI to write in a certain way, give it an example first. If you want it to write a poem that sounds like you, paste one of your poems first and say, "Write another one like this."

4.3 Ask for a Specific Tone

You can tell the AI to be "Funny," "Professional," "Strict," or "Friendly." This helps make sure the answer doesn't sound like a boring robot.

5. Real-Life Examples to Try

Here are three ways you can use these skills today:

  • For Students: "Act as a history tutor. Explain the importance of the ancient Benin Empire to a 10-year-old using simple words and a bulleted list."
  • For Aspiring Professionals: "Act as a career coach. Help me write a polite email to a company to ask if they have any internships. Keep it under 150 words."
  • For Trainers: "Act as an expert teacher. Help me create a 4-week lesson plan for a beginner photography class. Format it as a table."

6. Common Mistakes and "Lies" to Watch For

AI is amazing, but it isn't perfect. Sometimes, it makes things up. We call this a "Confident Guess" (or a hallucination). Because the AI is just trying to guess the next word, it might invent a date, a name, or a book that doesn't exist.

Always double-check the facts! If the AI gives you a phone number or a historical date, look it up on a trusted website to make sure it is real.

Also, don't treat AI like a Search Engine. On Google, you type "weather Lagos." With AI, you should say, "Tell me what the weather is like in Lagos today and if I should carry an umbrella."

7. Why This Helps Your Career

In the past, you had to learn a special computer language (like code) to make machines do big things. Today, you only need to learn how to use *your* language well.

Whether you are a student, a business owner, or a retiree looking to learn new things, AI can be your partner. It doesn't replace your brain; it gives your brain a powerful helper. Learning to prompt is like learning to use a calculator for the first time—it makes everything faster and easier.

To understand the deeper side of how this works, we can look at "Tokens." Computers don't see words like we do. They see small chunks of text called tokens. A short word might be one token, while a long word might be three. The AI calculates the mathematical chance of which token comes next. This is why changing one word in your request can change the whole answer.

There is also a setting called "Temperature." This is like a "Creativity Dial." If the temperature is low, the AI stays very factual (good for math). If the temperature is high, the AI gets very creative (good for writing poems). Even if you can't see the dial, your words can turn it. Telling the AI to "be creative" is like turning the dial up.

"Chain of Thought" is another important idea. By asking the AI to "think out loud," we help it check its own work. If it writes down the first step correctly, it is much more likely to get the second step right. This reduces mistakes in things like coding or legal work.

The history of this field began with "Large Language Models." Early versions were very hard to talk to. You had to use very specific, almost "magic" words. Modern models are much better at following simple human orders. This makes it easier for everyone to use, not just computer scientists.

In a professional setting, we are moving toward "Pipelines." This is when you use one AI answer to help start the next task. For example, one prompt summarizes a meeting, and a second prompt takes that summary to write a list of tasks for the team. Mastering these multi-step plans is what separates beginners from experts.

Ethics are also key. We must always use AI responsibly. This means not trying to break the AI's safety rules or using it to create harmful content. Good prompting is about being productive and helpful. At Sabicrest, we teach "Safe AI," showing students how to be leaders in their community by using technology for good.

The future of AI is "Multimodal." This means you won't just use text. You will be able to show the AI a photo and ask, "What is wrong with this car engine?" or give it a sound clip to translate. The core rules of being clear and giving context will stay the same no matter what.

Ultimately, prompting is about "Mapping." The AI has a giant map of all human knowledge. Your request is like the coordinates that tell the AI where to go. If your coordinates are slightly off, you end up in the wrong place. If they are perfect, you find the exact treasure you were looking for. This is why we call it a skill—it is a repeatable way to find the best information possible.

In conclusion, the technology is complex, but the practice is simple: Be clear, be specific, and give the AI a role. By doing this, you turn a machine into a partner. The future is not humans against machines; it is humans using machines to do more.
