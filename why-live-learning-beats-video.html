WHY LEARNING LIVE
BEATS WATCHING VIDEOS.

A simple look at why being in a real-time class helps you learn faster and get a better job than just watching old recordings.

1. The Two Ways to Learn Online

When you want to learn a new skill online, there are usually two paths you can take.

The first path is Recorded Learning (also called "self-paced"). This is when you buy a course that consists of videos and text files. You watch them whenever you want, all by yourself.

The second path is Live Learning. This is what we do at Sabicrest. You join a class at a specific time with a real teacher and other students. It’s like a real-world classroom, but on your computer.

While recorded videos are flexible, most people find it hard to stay motivated when they are alone. Live classes create a schedule and a community that keeps you excited and moving forward.

2. The Problem with Recorded Courses

Have you ever bought an online course and never finished it? You aren't alone. Experts call this the "Zombie Learning" crisis.

Real Statistic: According to a major MIT study, only about 3% to 6% of people actually finish recorded online courses.

In contrast, over 85% of people who join live classes finish them. That is a massive difference!

The reason is simple: when you learn alone, it’s easy to say, "I'll watch it tomorrow." But tomorrow never comes. In a live class, you have a date and a time to show up, which stops you from putting it off.

3. Why Your Brain Loves Live Classes

Our brains are built for social interaction. We learn better when we see and talk to other people.

3.1 Learning by Watching

When you watch a teacher solve a problem live, your brain actually "copies" what they are doing. In a recorded video, things are often edited to look perfect. In a live class, you see the teacher make small mistakes and fix them. This "seeing how to fix things" is actually where the most important learning happens.

3.2 Staying Focused

Knowing that a teacher might ask you a question keeps your brain awake and alert. Scientists have found that this mild "social pressure" helps your brain store information much more effectively. When you just watch a video, your mind might wander to what's for dinner, but in a live class, you stay "in the zone."

Did you know? A study in the PNAS journal showed that students in "active" learning environments (like live labs) score half a letter grade higher than those in traditional lectures.

4. Learning with a "Squad"

At Sabicrest, you join a small group of people who are learning exactly what you are. We call this a "Cohort" or your "Squad."

  • Friends Keep You Going: When you see your friends showing up every week, you want to show up too. You help each other finish projects.
  • No Hiding: In a live class, you can't just hide in the back. Breakout rooms and group tasks mean you get to participate and share your ideas.
  • Making Connections: You meet people from different cities and even different countries. These people often become the friends who help you find your next job.

5. Getting Help Right Away

Imagine you are learning how to build an app and you get stuck on Step 2.

In a recorded course, you have to stop, search Google, or post on a forum and wait two days for an answer. By the time someone replies, you’ve lost your excitement.

In a Live Session, you just raise your hand. The teacher answers you right then and there. You can even share your screen so they can show you exactly what to click. This "Instant Feedback" means you never stay stuck for long.

6. How it Helps You Get a Job

Companies today don't just want to see a certificate; they want to see that you can work in a team and meet deadlines.

When you finish a live program, you prove that you can show up on time, communicate with others, and finish work by a certain date. These are the "hidden" skills that managers look for.

Also, you learn much faster. A recorded course might take you a whole year to finish because you keep stopping. A live class gets you to your goal in just 8 to 12 weeks. This means you can start earning money much sooner.

7. What the Future Holds

In a world full of AI and robots, the most valuable thing you have is your human connection.

Information is everywhere for free, but mentorship is rare. The future of learning isn't just about watching a screen; it's about building relationships with experts who can guide you through the noise.

At Sabicrest, we believe that the best way to learn is with people, for people. We are building the future of work by making learning live, fun, and human.

For those who want to go deeper, let's talk about the "Zone of Proximal Development." This is a fancy term for finding the perfect level of challenge. Live learning is the only way to adjust this in real-time. A teacher can see if a class is confused and change their explanation immediately. This is something a video file simply cannot do.

We also have to watch out for the "Illusion of Competence." When you watch a video, it feels easy because the teacher is doing all the work. You think you know it, but you don't. It's only when you are forced to build something live that you realize what you actually understand. This is why our live labs are so important—they push you to actually "do" the work.

Technology has also made live classes much better. With new protocols like WebRTC, we can have global classes with almost no delay. This means you can see the teacher's facial expressions and head nods, which helps build trust and understanding.

In places like Nigeria, where data and electricity cost a lot, "failing" a course is very expensive. It's much smarter to invest in a live class that has a 90% success rate than a cheap video course that almost everyone fails.

Skills are changing faster than ever. A video about AI from six months ago might already be out of date. Live classes allow for "Zero-Day Updates," meaning your mentor can talk about things that happened this morning. This keeps you at the very top of your field.

Finally, degrees are becoming less important than "Proof of Work." In our live classes, every week you produce something real—a piece of code or a design. These real-world projects are much more valuable to a boss than a piece of paper that says you watched some videos.
