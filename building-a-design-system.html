BUILDING A
DESIGN SYSTEM.

A simple guide to creating a collection of rules and tools that make building apps and websites faster and more consistent.

Contents
1. What is a Design System? 2. The Main Parts of a System 3. Why Do You Need One? 4. How to Start Building Your Own 5. Keeping the System Alive 6. The Future of Design Systems

1. What is a Design System?

A Design System is like a box of LEGO blocks for a company. Imagine if you wanted to build ten different LEGO houses. If you had to make every brick from scratch for every house, it would take forever. Instead, you have a set of standard bricks—red ones, blue ones, flat ones, and tall ones—that fit together perfectly every time.

In the world of technology, a design system is a collection of reusable parts (like buttons, fonts, and colors) and clear rules on how to use them. It ensures that no matter who is working on a project, the final product looks and feels like it belongs to the same family. It is much more than just a "style guide"; it is a living library that both designers and engineers use every day.

2. The Main Parts of a System

Design systems are usually broken down into smaller pieces. A common way to think about this is "Atomic Design," where you start with the smallest items and build up to bigger ones.

2.1 Foundations (The Basics)

These are the core ingredients of your brand. They include:

  • Colors: A specific palette of primary, secondary, and neutral colors.
  • Typography: The fonts you use, their sizes, and how much space is between lines.
  • Spacing: Rules for how much room should be between different elements.
  • Icons: A set of simple pictures that help users understand what to do.

2.2 Components (The Blocks)

These are the actual parts people interact with. Think of things like buttons, checkboxes, input fields, and navigation bars. Each component has different "states"—for example, what a button looks like when it's clicked versus when it's disabled.

3. Why Do You Need One?

As a company grows, it becomes harder to keep everything looking the same. Without a system, one designer might use a bright blue button, and another might use a dark blue one. This confuses users and makes the brand look unprofessional.

Key Benefit: Speed. Once a system is built, designers don't have to "draw" a button ever again. They just grab one from the library. Engineers don't have to "code" it again. This allows teams to build new features in days instead of weeks.

Consistency is another huge win. When users see the same patterns across an app, they learn how to use it faster. It creates a sense of trust and quality.

4. How to Start Building Your Own

Building a system is a big job, but you can start small. Here is a simple four-step process:

4.1 Audit Your Current Design

Look at your existing app or website. Take screenshots of every button, every font size, and every color. You will likely find that you are using 50 different shades of grey and 20 different button styles. This helps everyone see why a system is needed.

4.2 Define Your Standards

Sit down with your team and decide on the "winners." Which blue is our official brand blue? Which font should we use for headings? Document these decisions clearly.

4.3 Build the Library

In a design tool like Figma, create a master file where all these parts live. Then, work with your engineers to build those same parts in code. The goal is for the design library and the code library to match perfectly.

5. Keeping the System Alive

A design system is never "finished." It is a living product that needs a team to look after it. As the company changes, the system needs to change too. New components will be needed, and old ones will need to be updated.

Success happens when the system is easy to find and easy to use. If it's too complicated, people will go back to making things up from scratch. Good documentation (a website where people can see the components and how to use them) is key.

6. The Future of Design Systems

We are entering an exciting era of "Design Tokens" and "AI-driven Systems." Design tokens are simple variables (like a nickname for a color) that allow changes to happen across multiple platforms (iOS, Android, and Web) at the same time. AI will soon help us check if our designs follow our own system rules automatically.

At Sabicrest, we teach these modern workflows so our students are prepared to lead teams at the world's most innovative companies.

To provide a more comprehensive overview for professional readers, we must dive into the concept of "System Governance." Governance is the set of rules that determines how the system evolves. There are three main models: Centralized, Federated, and Hybrid. In a Centralized model, a dedicated team of designers and engineers manages the system for the entire company. This ensures high quality and consistency but can become a bottleneck. In a Federated model, designers from different product teams contribute to the system. This is great for variety but can lead to a messy library if not managed well. Most large companies, like Spotify or Airbnb, use a Hybrid model, where a small core team manages the foundations, but individual teams contribute new components.

Another critical technical area is "Accessibility" (often called a11y). A design system is the best place to solve accessibility problems once and for all. If you ensure your color palette has enough contrast and your buttons are large enough for someone with limited motor skills, every app you build with those parts will automatically be accessible. This is not just a moral duty; it is increasingly a legal requirement in many markets. At Sabicrest, we emphasize "Inclusive Design" in our curriculum, teaching students how to use the design system to serve everyone, regardless of their physical abilities.

Design Tokens are the "DNA" of a modern system. Instead of hard-coding a color like "#FF0000" in ten different places, you create a token called "color-action-primary." Designers use this token in Figma, and engineers use it in their code. If you ever want to change that color from red to blue, you only have to change it in one place. This creates "Single Source of Truth" that is incredibly powerful for scaling companies. It also allows for "Theming," where a user can switch between Light Mode and Dark Mode instantly because the system just swaps out one set of tokens for another.

When building a system for a Lagos-based company, we must also consider "Performance." Every component you add to the library adds weight to the final app. If the library is too bloated, it will slow down page load times on slower networks. A good system should be modular, allowing teams to only import the parts they actually need. This "Tree Shaking" approach is essential for providing a fast user experience in regions where internet speed is variable.

The concept of "Design Ops" (Design Operations) has emerged to help manage the people and processes around a design system. Design Ops focus on things like "Tooling," "Workflows," and "Culture." They ask questions like: How do we hand off designs to engineers? How do we measure if the system is actually saving time? How do we train new hires to use our specific blocks? As teams scale, these operational questions become just as important as the visual design itself.

Finally, we must mention "System ROI" (Return on Investment). How do you prove to a CEO that spending six months building a design system is worth it? You measure "Speed to Market" and "Design Debt." Companies with a robust system can launch new products up to 47% faster. They also spend much less time on "Visual QA"—the tedious process of checking if every pixel matches the original design. By saving hundreds of hours of manual labor, a design system pays for itself many times over.

The adoption of version control for design, similar to Git for engineers, has also revolutionized how systems are built. Tools like Figma now offer "Branching" and "Merging," allowing a designer to work on a new version of a component without breaking the main library. This ensures that the system is always in a stable state for the rest of the company to use.

Multi-brand design systems represent the peak of complexity in this field. Companies that own multiple brands (like Expedia Group or Google) need a system that defines shared logic but allows for unique visual expressions for each brand. This is achieved through a "Tiered Token System," where "Global Tokens" define sizes and spacing, but "Brand Tokens" define specific colors and fonts. Building this requires a deep understanding of software architecture and visual design.

In conclusion, building a design system is an investment in your company’s future. It turns design from a slow, manual process into a fast, automated engine. It allows your best people to stop worrying about what shade of blue to use and start worrying about how to solve your customers' biggest problems. That is the true power of a system.
