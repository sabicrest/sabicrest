MAKING WEB3
EASY FOR EVERYONE.

A simple guide to how we can make Web3 (the new internet) easy and fun for everyone to use. We'll talk about making things simple, cheap, and safe, so you don't have to be a computer expert to enjoy it.

1. What is UX in Web3?

"UX" stands for User Experience. It's all about how easy and enjoyable something is to use. Think about your favorite app or website – good UX makes it simple and clear. In Web3, UX is about making sure that using new internet tools (like apps that use blockchain) feels just as easy, even though the technology behind them is very new and often complicated.

Web3 is often called the "new internet" because it lets you truly own your digital items and data, unlike the older internet where big companies often owned everything. But right now, using Web3 can be a bit like trying to fly a spaceship without a pilot's license! Good Web3 UX aims to hide all the complicated buttons and levers, so you can enjoy the ride without needing to be an expert. We want to make it so you can use your digital wallet to talk to apps, manage your online identity, and sign things digitally, all without thinking about the complex computer code happening in the background. The big goal is "Invisible Web3" – where the amazing new technology works so smoothly, you don't even notice it's there.

2. How the Internet Has Changed

To understand where Web3 is going, let's take a quick look at how the internet has grown up over the years.

2.1 Web1: The "Read-Only" Internet (Like a Digital Book)

This was the internet from about 1990 to 2004. Think of it like a giant digital library. You could read websites, look at pictures, and find information, but you couldn't really interact much. It was mostly about consuming information. Making things easy to read and work on different computers was the main goal.

2.2 Web2: The "Social" Internet (Big Companies Rule)

From about 2004 until now, this is the internet most of us use every day. It's where you can create content, share photos, and chat with friends on social media (like Facebook, Instagram, or TikTok). But here's the catch: big companies own these platforms and your data. They decide the rules, and you often log in using your Google or Apple account. You trust these big companies with your information.

2.3 Web3: The "Ownership" Internet (You're in Charge!)

Web3 is the next big step. Instead of big companies owning your data and digital stuff, *you* do. It's built on special technology called blockchain, which is like a super-secure, shared record book. When you do something in Web3, it's often called a "transaction" (like sending money or buying a digital item). In the early days of Web3 (around 2015-2022), it was very hard to use. You needed to understand complicated computer codes and secret phrases (like "seed phrases") to keep your digital money safe. It was mostly for computer experts, not everyday people.

3. The Tricky Parts of Web3 Today

Right now, Web3 has some big "headaches" that make it hard for regular people to use. These are things that make you scratch your head and say, "Why is this so complicated?" Designers call these "friction points." They go against what we expect from easy-to-use apps.

Here are some of the main tricky parts:

  • Key Custody: The requirement to manage 12-to-24-word mnemonics creates a "Single Point of Failure" for the user.
  • Gas Mechanics: Requiring the native token of a network (e.g., ETH) to perform any action creates a high barrier to entry for new participants.
  • Latency: Traditional databases update in milliseconds; blockchains require network consensus, leading to "Finality Lag" which confuses users accustomed to instant feedback.
  • Irreversibility: The immutable nature of blockchain transactions means an error in an address or amount results in permanent loss of value.

3.1 Secret Passwords (Seed Phrases)

Imagine having a secret password that is 12 to 24 random words long. If you lose it, you lose all your digital money and items forever! If someone else finds it, they can take everything. This is called a "seed phrase," and it's how you control your digital wallet. It's a huge responsibility and very scary for most people.

3.2 Paying for Every Little Thing (Gas Fees)

Every time you do something on a blockchain (like sending money or buying a digital picture), you have to pay a small fee, often in a special digital currency (like ETH). This is called "gas." It's like paying a tiny toll for every single action. It can be confusing, expensive, and a big barrier for new users.

3.3 Waiting Around (Slow Speeds)

When you use a regular app, things happen instantly. In Web3, because many computers around the world have to agree on what just happened, things can sometimes be slow. You might click a button and have to wait a few seconds or even minutes for it to finish. This "waiting time" can be frustrating and confusing.

3.4 No Undo Button (Permanent Mistakes)

Once you do something on a blockchain, it's usually permanent. If you accidentally send money to the wrong address, or buy the wrong digital item, there's no customer service to call and no "undo" button. Your money or item is gone forever. This makes people very nervous and careful.

4. Making Wallets Smarter (Account Abstraction)

This is one of the biggest and most exciting changes coming to Web3! Imagine your digital wallet becoming much smarter, almost like a mini-computer itself. This big upgrade is called "Account Abstraction" (or EIP-4337, if you want the technical name). It means your wallet can do much more than just hold money; it can follow rules and even have friends help you.

4.1 Getting Your Wallet Back (Social Recovery)

Remember those scary 12-to-24-word secret phrases? With smarter wallets, you might not need them! Instead, you could pick a few trusted friends or family members (called "Guardians"). If you lose access to your wallet, these guardians can help you get it back, without ever seeing your secret phrase. This takes away a huge fear of losing everything.

4.2 Free or Cheaper Actions (No Gas Fees!)

Imagine using a Web3 app without having to worry about those annoying "gas fees" for every click. With smarter wallets, a company or even another user could pay the gas fees for you! This means you could use Web3 apps for free, or pay for things with regular money (like Naira or Dollars) instead of needing a special digital currency. This makes Web3 much more welcoming for new people.

4.3 Doing Many Things at Once (Transaction Batching)

Right now, if you want to do a few things in a Web3 app (like approving an item, then buying it, then sending it to a friend), you often have to click "approve" many times. It's like having to pay for each grocery item separately at the checkout. Smarter wallets will let you bundle all these actions into one single approval. This saves time, clicks, and often money!

5. Knowing What's Happening (Feedback)

Since there's no "undo" button in Web3, it's super important that you always know exactly what's going on. Good Web3 design will give you clear "feedback" at every step, so you're never left guessing.

5.1 Clearer Messages (What You're Really Signing)

Have you ever seen a pop-up on your computer that asks you to agree to something, but it's full of confusing computer code? That's what many Web3 apps look like right now. In the future, your wallet will show you exactly what you're agreeing to in plain, easy-to-understand words. No more guessing if you're sending money or giving away your digital cat!

5.2 Seeing Before You Leap (Practice Runs)

Imagine if, before you sent money or bought something in Web3, the app could show you exactly what would happen. It would say, "If you click 'confirm,' your balance will go from 100 coins to 95 coins, and this digital item will appear in your wallet." This "practice run" or "simulation" helps you avoid mistakes and feel much safer, just like when your computer asks, "Are you sure you want to delete this file?"

5.3 Making Things Feel Fast (Quick Updates)

Even if the blockchain is a little slow, good design can make things *feel* fast. For example, when you send a message on WhatsApp, it shows "sending..." then "sent" very quickly, even if it takes a moment to reach the other person. Web3 apps will do the same, showing you quick updates on your screen so you don't have to wait around wondering if your action worked.

6. What's Next for Web3?

Looking ahead to the next few years, the lines between the "old internet" (Web2) and the "new internet" (Web3) will likely blur. We'll just have one amazing internet experience that combines the best of both worlds. The most successful Web3 tools will be those that let you start simple (maybe even with your regular email login) and then give you more control over your digital stuff as you get more comfortable. This is called "Progressive Decentralization" – slowly giving you more power as you learn.

Imagine Artificial Intelligence (AI) helping you use Web3. Instead of clicking many buttons to find the best way to save your digital money, you could just tell the AI, "I want to earn 5% on my digital dollars." The AI would then do all the complicated steps for you, finding the best apps and handling all the digital signatures and fees. This is called "Intent-Centric Design" – where you just say what you want, and the computer figures out how to do it.

In short, the future of Web3 isn't about making everyone a blockchain expert. It's about building tools that are so good and so easy to use that you don't even realize you're using blockchain technology. You just enjoy the benefits of owning your digital world, safely and simply.

Let's dive a bit deeper into some of the clever technical solutions that are making Web3 easier to use.

The Bundler: Your Transaction Helper

In Section 4, we talked about how smart wallets can help with gas fees. This is often done with something called a "Bundler." Imagine you want to send a letter, but you don't have a stamp. A Bundler is like a helpful post office worker who collects many letters from different people, puts all the stamps on them, and sends them all at once. For Web3, a Bundler collects many "UserOperations" (which are like your requests to the blockchain) and bundles them into one big transaction. This means you don't need to hold the special digital currency (like ETH) just to pay for a tiny action. The Bundler or another service (a "Paymaster") handles that for you. This is a big change from how things used to work, where you always had to pay for your own "stamps."

Super-Fast and Cheap Transactions (ZK-SNARKs)

Another amazing technology making Web3 better is called "Recursive ZK-SNARKs." Don't worry about the complicated name! Just think of it as a super-smart way to make thousands of small transactions into one tiny, super-fast, and super-cheap proof. This is used in special "Layer 2" networks (like zkSync and Starknet) that sit on top of the main blockchain. For you, the user, this means you can do things for almost no cost – sometimes less than a penny! The challenge for designers is to explain things like "withdrawal periods" (where your money might be temporarily locked for a few days) in a way that makes sense, so you don't get confused or worried.

The Invisible Bridge: Moving Money Between Blockchains

Imagine you have digital money on one blockchain (like Ethereum) but want to use it on another (like Polygon). Right now, it's like trying to move money between two different banks in different countries – it can be complicated. The "Invisible Bridge" is a future goal where you can simply click "buy" on a digital item on Polygon, and your wallet will automatically handle all the tricky steps: swapping your Ethereum, moving it to Polygon, and then buying the item, all with just one click. You won't see the complex "bridge" working in the background, just a simple progress bar. This is what we teach our engineers at Sabicrest – how to build these seamless experiences.

Safer Permissions (Permit-based Interactions)

To keep your digital wallet safe, there's a new way of giving permission to apps called "Permit-based interactions" (EIP-2612). In the past, you might have given an app "infinite approval" to spend your digital money, which was risky if that app ever got hacked. Now, you can give permission for a specific amount, for a specific time, and it expires automatically. This is much safer! Designers need to make sure these new permissions are shown clearly, so you always know exactly what you're allowing and for how long.

Unlocking with Your Face or Finger (Biometric Signing)

The ultimate goal for ease of use is to get rid of all those complicated passwords and seed phrases. Imagine unlocking your digital wallet and approving transactions with just your face (FaceID) or your fingerprint (TouchID), just like you unlock your phone! This is called "Biometric Signing." It makes Web3 super easy and secure, because your phone's built-in security protects your digital identity. When our students at Sabicrest learn to build this, they are removing one of the last big hurdles for everyone to use Web3.

Making Decisions Together (Governance UX)

Many Web3 projects are run by their users, like a digital club where everyone gets a vote. These are called DAOs (Decentralized Autonomous Organizations). But sometimes, voting can be complicated and expensive. Future designs will make it easy to vote, or even let you choose an expert to vote for you. It will also show you clearly what your vote will change, so you can make smart decisions.

Designing with Care (Ethical Design)

Because Web3 can involve real money, it's very important to design apps ethically. This means not tricking people into making risky investments or encouraging them to gamble. Good Web3 designers will add features like "cooling-off periods" for big transactions or clear warnings for risky trades. At Sabicrest, we teach our students to always put the user's well-being first, building products that are helpful and safe, not just exciting.

The Big Picture: Simple, Safe, and Powerful

When we put all these ideas together, the future of Web3 UX is about combining clever computer science, smart ways to manage information, and a deep understanding of how people think. It's about making really complex technology feel completely normal. As more and more people start using Web3, the technical details will fade into the background. What will be left is an internet that gives you more privacy, more security, and more power over your own digital life than ever before. This is the exciting promise of Web3, and it all depends on how well we design it to be easy for everyone.

Keeping Things Fast (State Bloat)

One challenge for blockchains is that they keep growing bigger and bigger with every transaction. This "State Bloat" can sometimes make apps slow. To fix this, developers are using smart tricks like "Light Clients" and "Verkle Trees." These allow your phone or computer to quickly check what's happening on the blockchain without having to download the entire massive history. This means Web3 apps can stay fast, even for people with slower internet or older phones.

Trusting the Code (Semantic Trust)

On the old internet, you trust a big company because of its brand name. In Web3, you're supposed to trust the computer code itself. But most people can't read computer code! So, UX designers need to create "Semantic Trust." This means using clear pictures, easy-to-understand messages, and showing you that other people in the community have checked and approved the app. It's about building trust through clear communication and transparency, so you feel safe even if you don't understand every line of code. This is a key part of what we teach at Sabicrest, preparing students to build a trustworthy digital future.
